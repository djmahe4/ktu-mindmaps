Introduction

This guide covers the fundamentals of professional software development, including ethics, processes, and methodologies. Use the interactive sections below to learn and test your knowledge.

Core Idea: Think of a skilled Doctor – their work requires deep professionalism, strict ethics, and a defined process to ensure patient safety and success. The same principles apply in software engineering.

1. Waterfall Model

A linear-sequential life cycle model where each phase must be completed before the next phase can begin.

Phases:
  • Requirement Gathering & Feasibility Analysis: Define and document requirements and assess project viability.
  • Design: Create the system architecture and software design based on requirements.
  • Coding/Implementation: Write the code for the software.
  • Testing: Verify and validate the software, finding and fixing defects.
  • Deployment: Release the software to the users.
  • Maintenance: Provide ongoing support and enhancements.
Advantages:
  • Simple and easy to understand.
  • Phases are clearly defined.
  • Good for smaller projects with clear requirements.
Disadvantages:
  • Inflexible; difficult to accommodate changes.
  • Testing occurs late, increasing risk.
  • Poor model for long, complex projects.
Mnemonic: Water only flows down, not back up → No going back easily!

2. Incremental Development

Combines elements of the waterfall model applied in an iterative fashion. The product is designed, implemented, and tested incrementally until it is finished.

Advantages:
  • Delivers functional parts of the system early.
  • More flexible to changing requirements.
  • Easier to manage risk.
Disadvantages:
  • Requires good planning and design.
  • Integration of increments can be complex.
Mnemonic: Think of building with Lego blocks – you build and deliver a usable piece step-by-step.

3. Prototyping

Advantages:
  • Users get early experience of the system.
  • Reduces risk of requirement misunderstandings.
Disadvantages:
  • Can lead to poorly structured systems.
  • Users may mistake the prototype for the final system.
Mnemonic: A prototype is a "practice version" before the final match.

4. Boehm's Spiral Model

A risk-driven process model generator. It combines features of the prototyping model and the waterfall model, and is advantageous for large, complex projects.

Phases (Quadrants in each loop):
  • Determine objectives, alternatives, and constraints: Define goals for the phase, identify constraints, and plan management.
  • Evaluate alternatives, identify and resolve risks: Analyze risks and take steps to reduce them, e.g., by prototyping.
  • Develop and verify next-level product: Choose a development model and build the next version of the product.
  • Plan the next phase: Review the project and decide whether to proceed with another loop of the spiral.
Advantages:
  • Strong risk analysis at every phase.
  • Good for large, high-risk projects.
Disadvantages:
  • Expensive and time-consuming.
  • Requires expertise in risk assessment.
Mnemonic: A spiral keeps looping around, with risk checks at every turn.

5. Agile Methods & Manifesto

Advantages:
  • Quick delivery of usable software.
  • Encourages customer collaboration.
  • Adaptable to changes.
Disadvantages:
  • Hard to predict cost/time for large projects.
  • Less documentation can create future issues.
Mnemonic: Agile means being "quick & flexible" like a gymnast.
